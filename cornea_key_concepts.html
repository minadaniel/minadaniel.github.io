🔬 Corneal Anatomy & Physiology

The six layers, their clinical significance, and why the cornea is transparent

The Six Layers — What Each One Means Clinically

Epithelium (50 µm, 5–6 cell layers)

Regenerates completely every 7–10 days from limbal stem cells at the Palisades of Vogt. This is why an abrasion heals in 48–72 hours. The stem cells are the factory — destroy them and the conjunctiva grows onto the cornea (conjunctivalisation = limbal stem cell deficiency / LSCD).

Patients with severe chemical burns, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, or multiple surgeries can deplete limbal stem cells → the corneal surface becomes chronically unstable and vascularised. Treatment: limbal stem cell transplantation (from fellow eye or living relative).

Bowman's Layer (12–15 µm, acellular)

Cannot regenerate — any injury leaves a permanent opacity/scar. This is clinically important for dystrophies that target Bowman's (Reis-Bücklers, Thiel-Behnke) — they cause irreversible haze. PTK can ablate superficial Bowman's opacities, but the layer itself never reforms.

Stroma (~500 µm — 90% of total thickness)

Made of regular parallel collagen lamellae (types I and V) maintained by keratocytes. The precise 60 nm spacing between lamellae produces destructive interference of scattered light — this is what makes the stroma transparent. Any disruption (oedema, scar, infiltrate) breaks the regular spacing → opacity.

In keratoconus: collagen fibres are thinner and disorganised → structural weakness → ectasia under normal IOP.

Dua's Layer (pre-Descemet's, ~15 µm) — identified 2013

A distinct acellular layer between stroma and Descemet's. Clinically relevant for DALK surgery: Anwar's big-bubble technique targets the plane between stroma and Dua's layer. Type 1 bubble (between stroma and Dua's) = ideal. Type 2 bubble (between Dua's and DM) = less ideal but still usable.

Descemet's Membrane (10–12 µm)

Basement membrane of the endothelium. Secreted throughout life — thickens with age (~3 µm at birth, ~10–12 µm in adults). Rupture → acute hydrops (aqueous floods stroma → sudden white opacification in advanced KC). In Fuchs dystrophy, abnormal collagen deposits form guttae on its posterior surface.

Endothelium (single layer of ~500,000 cells, ~5 µm)

The most important layer for corneal clarity. Normal density: 2,500–3,000 cells/mm². Critical threshold: ~500–700 cells/mm² — below this the pump fails and the cornea becomes permanently oedematous.

Human endothelial cells cannot divide (arrested in G1 phase). Lost cells are replaced by neighbouring cells spreading and enlarging (polymegethism = unequal size; pleomorphism = irregular shape) — seen on specular microscopy as a warning sign of endothelial stress.

Function: actively pumps Na⁺ out of stroma (Na/K-ATPase + carbonic anhydrase driven) → maintains corneal dehydration (normal water content ~78%). Without this active pumping the stroma would absorb aqueous and become oedematous.

📌 The endothelium is a PUMP, not a barrier. When it fails, fluid accumulates from the inside. This is why Fuchs patients have morning blur — overnight, without evaporation from a closed eye, the failing pump cannot compensate, and fluid accumulates.

Why Is the Cornea Transparent?

Three simultaneous requirements — loss of any one causes opacity:

  1. Avascularity — blood vessels scatter light and carry inflammatory cells. The limbal vessels terminate at the corneal margin. Anything that induces corneal vascularisation (chronic hypoxia from CL, chemical burns, severe dry eye) threatens transparency.
  2. Regular collagen spacing — lamellae at ~60 nm intervals cancel scattered light by destructive interference. Oedema pushes fibres apart; scars disorganise them — both cause opacity.
  3. Relative dehydration — maintained by the endothelial pump. Normal water content ~78%; if this rises to ~85%+ the stroma clouds.

Corneal Sensation

The cornea is the most densely innervated tissue in the body — ophthalmic branch of CN V (trigeminal) via the nasociliary nerve → long ciliary nerves → sub-basal nerve plexus (visible on confocal microscopy). Sensation serves two purposes: (1) protective blink reflex and (2) neurotrophic support — corneal nerves release substance P and nerve growth factor (NGF) that directly maintain epithelial health. This is why CN V damage (not CN VII) causes neurotrophic keratopathy.

⚠️ The distinction between CN V and CN VII damage causes constant confusion. CN V = sensation (trigeminal) = neurotrophic keratopathy when damaged. CN VII = eyelid closure (facial nerve) = exposure/lagophthalmos keratopathy when damaged. Two completely different entities with different mechanisms and different treatments.

🔺 Keratoconus & Ectasia

Progressive non-inflammatory corneal thinning and protrusion — most common ectasia, most common corneal transplant indication under 40

Keratoconus (KC) begins insidiously in late teens, progresses through the 20s and 30s, then typically stabilises. The typical presenting story: a young patient whose glasses prescription keeps changing (myopia + astigmatism increasing), who never quite gets good vision even with new glasses, and whose retinoscopy shows a "scissors" movement instead of a clean reflex.

Clinical Signs

Vogt's Striae

Fine vertical lines in the deep posterior stroma caused by mechanical stress from the cone. The pathognomonic test: they disappear transiently with gentle digital pressure on the globe (briefly equalising the stress). Post-traumatic or other stromal lines do not disappear with pressure. Best seen on slit lamp with direct narrow beam or retroillumination.

Fleischer Ring

Haemosiderin (iron) deposited in the basal epithelium at the base of the cone due to disruption of normal tear film distribution. Characteristically incomplete (not a full 360° ring). Best seen with cobalt blue filter. Key distinction from PMD: PMD does NOT have a Fleischer ring — it only occurs where there is a cone (KC, Keratoglobus not PMD).

Munson's Sign

The cone protrudes far enough to push the lower eyelid into a V-shape on downgaze. This is a late, advanced sign — if present, the KC is clinically significant. Ask the patient to look straight down at the floor and observe the lower lid contour from the side.

Oil-Droplet / Scissors Reflex

On retinoscopy: the cone deflects the retinoscopy reflex so it moves in a "scissors" pattern (two lobes moving in opposite directions) instead of a uniform sweep. On direct ophthalmoscopy: a dark shadow ring around a brighter central area ("oil droplet") as the cone displaces the reflex. This is often the first clinical suspicion of KC — during routine refraction in a young patient.

💡 Wessely ring = immune ring (antigen-antibody complexes in mid-stroma) seen in viral/bacterial keratitis. It is NOT a sign of KC. Distinguish carefully — this comes up in nearly every KC exam question.

Understanding the Pentacam

The Pentacam uses a rotating Scheimpflug camera to build a 3D model of the cornea from multiple slit images. It generates four key maps that together tell you whether a cornea is safe for refractive surgery or showing early ectasia.

Map 1 — Axial (Sagittal) Curvature

Shows average curvature at each point, referenced to the corneal apex. Colours = dioptric power: red/orange = steep (high K), blue = flat (low K). Normal central K = 41–46D. In KC: characteristic inferior steepening — an island of warm colour inferiorly. Most intuitive map for spotting KC.

Map 2 — Tangential (Instantaneous) Curvature

Shows the local rate of curvature change — more sensitive to sudden transitions in surface shape than the axial map (which averages over a larger area and smooths small changes). Better for detecting peripheral ectasia, early changes, and for contact lens fitting. Looks more alarming than axial in early KC — don't let this mislead you.

Map 3 — Pachymetry Map

Corneal thickness at every point. Normal: thickest at periphery (~650–700 µm), thinning centrally (~520–560 µm). In KC: thinnest point shifts inferiorly and the peripheral-to-central thinning gradient is steeper than normal. Critical for surgery: need ≥400 µm for safe CXL, ≥250 µm RSB after LASIK ablation.

Map 4 — Elevation Maps (Front & Back)

Height of the corneal surface above/below a "best-fit sphere" — a mathematical sphere that best matches the patient's cornea. Warm colours (red) = elevated above the sphere (the cone protrudes). The posterior elevation map is the most sensitive indicator of early ectasia — the back surface deforms before the front. Suspicious: posterior elevation >16 µm above best-fit sphere. Normal: ≤12 µm anterior, ≤15 µm posterior elevation above BFS.

📌 Posterior elevation is the earliest Pentacam finding in subclinical (forme fruste) KC. A front map that looks nearly normal but has a posterior elevation island >16 µm warrants serious caution before LASIK. This is the most clinically important Pentacam concept for refractive surgery screening.
BAD-D Score (Belin-Ambrosio Enhanced Ectasia Display)

A composite score that compares the patient's values against a normative database across all maps simultaneously. BAD-D >1.6 = suspicious for ectasia — most surgeons use this as the final safety gate. Combines: front/back elevation deviation, pachymetric progression index, and overall deviation from normal population. Advantages: catches borderline cases that look almost normal on individual maps.

Keratoconus vs Pellucid Marginal Degeneration

Keratoconus
  • Thinning at the apex of the cone
  • Protrusion at the thinning point (same location)
  • Central or paracentral location
  • Fleischer ring present
  • Vogt's striae present
  • Topography: inferior steepening
  • Oil-droplet reflex on retinoscopy
Pellucid Marginal Degeneration
  • Thinning = inferior band, 1–2 mm from limbus
  • Protrusion ABOVE the thinning ← key distinction
  • Clear zone between thinning and limbus (hallmark)
  • No Fleischer ring
  • No Vogt's striae
  • Topography: butterfly / crab-claw pattern
  • Severe against-the-rule astigmatism
🧠 PMD: Protrusion is Missing from the thinning zone — it's above it, not at it. The band thins but doesn't protrude; the cornea above it buckles forward.

Acute Hydrops

What Happens

In advanced KC, Descemet's membrane (DM) can suddenly rupture. Aqueous from the anterior chamber rushes through the tear into the stroma → dramatic acute stromal oedema → sudden white opacification of the cornea + vision drop. The patient experiences sudden severe visual loss, often with some pain. The eye looks white/grey in the corneal region.

Management — NOT an Emergency for Surgery

Conservative first — DM will heal on its own over 6–10 weeks:

  • Cycloplegic (e.g. cyclopentolate) for ciliary spasm
  • Hypertonic saline 5% drops (osmotically draws water from oedematous stroma)
  • Lubricants and BCL for comfort
  • Reassurance — the stroma will clear once DM heals

Intracameral gas injection (C3F8 or SF6): newer technique — gas bubble pushed against the DM tear accelerates healing. Shortens duration from ~10 weeks to ~3–5 weeks.

After resolution: scar usually remains → reassess for keratoplasty at ~4–6 months (wait for oedema to fully resolve).

⚠️ Acute hydrops is NOT an indication for emergency keratoplasty. Operating on an acutely inflamed, oedematous cornea leads to poor graft outcomes. Wait for resolution — this is a classic exam and clinical trap.

🟤 Corneal Degenerations & Iron Lines

Understanding the mechanism makes the locations logical — all iron lines share one principle

Iron Lines — One Mechanism, Four Locations

Haemosiderin (iron) from the tear film deposits in the basal epithelium wherever surface irregularity disrupts normal tear flow and iron pools. All four lines are epithelial level — none are at Descemet's membrane. All are best seen with cobalt blue filter on the slit lamp.

💡 The Kayser-Fleischer ring of Wilson's disease is COPPER deposited at Descemet's membrane — completely different mechanism, different level, different disease. Do not confuse with Fleischer ring of KC.

Band Keratopathy

Calcium phosphate deposition in Bowman's layer, limited to the interpalpebral zone (3–9 o'clock) because this exposed area has CO₂ evaporation that raises local pH → calcium phosphate precipitates at higher pH.

Three Distinguishing Features

  1. Lucid interval from the limbus — calcium spares the very peripheral edge where Bowman's layer ends at the limbal connective tissue
  2. Swiss-cheese holes within the band — perforations where corneal nerves pass through Bowman's (nerves are resistant to calcium deposition)
  3. Interpalpebral location — follows the palpebral fissure, not the entire circumference

Treatment

  • EDTA chelation (first-line): Na₂EDTA 0.37% applied to de-epithelialised cornea — chelates and removes calcium ions. Quick, clinic-based, inexpensive.
  • PTK: For recurrent or dense cases — excimer laser ablates the superficial Bowman's layer.

Terrien's Marginal Degeneration

Terrien's Marginal Degeneration
  • Starts superiorly, spreads circumferentially
  • Intact epithelium — no fluorescein staining
  • Lipid deposits at leading edge (yellow-white)
  • Progressive peripheral thinning → severe irregular astigmatism
  • Painless, bilateral, male predominance

  • Starts inferiorly, spreads circumferentially

⚠️ Terrien's has INTACT epithelium — no fluorescein staining of the thinned zone. This is the single most testable feature. Mooren's = ulcer (stains). Terrien's = thinning without ulceration.

Peripheral Ulcerative Keratitis (PUK)

PUK is peripheral corneal ulceration (with epithelial defect + fluorescein staining) associated with systemic vasculitis. The peripheral cornea is vascularised — immune complexes circulating in systemic disease deposit here and activate complement → local inflammation → collagenase release → corneal melt.

🧬 Corneal Dystrophies

Inherited, bilateral, symmetric, non-inflammatory — classified by anatomical layer (IC3D classification)

Dystrophies are genetic conditions causing corneal opacification or dysfunction without prior infection or systemic disease. The most practical classification is by layer — this immediately tells you what surgery is needed.

Why Macular Looks So Different

Granular and Lattice produce discrete deposits in an otherwise relatively normal-looking stroma. Macular produces diffuse accumulation of keratan sulphate throughout the entire stroma — even the ground substance between opacities is abnormally opaque. This is also why Macular extends to the limbus (disease is pan-stromal) while Granular and Lattice spare the periphery.

Macular is also unique in involving the full thickness including Descemet's and endothelium — so it has the worst prognosis and highest graft recurrence rate.

Fuchs Endothelial Dystrophy

The most common indication for endothelial keratoplasty in the West. You will see it regularly — typically a woman in her 60s–70s with blurry vision that is worst in the morning and improves through the day.

Why Is Vision Worst in the Morning?

During sleep the eyes are closed → no evaporation from the ocular surface → the failing endothelium cannot keep up with the extra fluid load → stroma accumulates more water overnight → greater oedema on waking. Over the day, evaporation from the open eye removes some fluid → vision improves. This diurnal variation (worse AM, better PM) is essentially pathognomonic for Fuchs.

As the disease advances, oedema persists all day, then bullae form → pain (ruptured bullae expose bare nerve endings) → eventually sub-epithelial fibrosis forms (paradoxically painless as nerves are destroyed).

Guttae — Where They Start and Why It Matters

Guttae (singular gutta = drop) are wart-like excrescences of abnormal collagen deposited on the posterior surface of Descemet's membrane by failing endothelial cells. On specular microscopy they appear as dark holes (non-reflective) giving a "beaten metal" appearance.

Critical fact: In Fuchs, guttae start CENTRALLY and spread towards the periphery. This distinguishes them from Hassall-Henle bodies = peripheral guttae found in normal elderly individuals (age-related, no clinical significance). When you see a patient with peripheral guttae only, do NOT diagnose Fuchs — this is normal ageing.

📌 Central guttae = early Fuchs (requires monitoring and counselling). Peripheral guttae only (Hassall-Henle bodies) = normal ageing. The location determines the significance.

🦠 Infectious Keratitis

Bacterial, fungal, viral, and protozoal — recognise each by its context and slit lamp appearance

Bacterial Keratitis

The corneal epithelium is normally an effective barrier. Risk factors that breach it: contact lens wear (hypoxia, microtrauma, biofilm formation), trauma, pre-existing corneal disease, dry eye, and post-surgical exposure.

Fungal Keratitis

Underdiagnosed because it mimics bacterial keratitis. The key context: corneal ulcer following vegetable/plant trauma not responding to antibiotics.

Filamentous (Fusarium, Aspergillus)
  • Trauma with plant/soil/vegetable matter
  • Feathery, irregular, fluffy borders
  • Satellite lesions (separate infiltrate islands)
  • Endothelial plaque (fungus growing behind DM)
  • Dry, raised surface on slit lamp
  • Natamycin 5% — first-line
  • Voriconazole 1% — better penetration, for deep/severe
Yeast (Candida)
  • Immunocompromised, chronic steroid use, post-surgery
  • More like bacterial — rounder, smoother borders
  • Less satellite lesions
  • Voriconazole 1% — first-line
  • Amphotericin B 0.15% — alternative
📌 Natamycin = filamentous fungi. Amphotericin B = yeast (Candida). This is commonly reversed in answers. Remember: Natamycin is topically insoluble (suspension) and doesn't penetrate well — suitable for filamentous infections which tend to be more superficial. Voriconazole penetrates better and is preferred for deep/posterior infections.

HSV Keratitis

The Dendrite — Understanding the Difference Between True and Pseudo
  • HSV (true dendrite): True epithelial ulcer. Fluorescein stains the base (exposed basement membrane). Rose bengal/lissamine stains the edges (virus-laden border cells). Terminal bulbs at tips — pathognomonic for HSV. Reduced corneal sensation.
  • HZV (pseudodendrite): Elevated mucous plaque — NOT an ulcer. No terminal bulbs. Rose bengal stains the entire plaque. Associated with dermatomal skin rash.
  • Acanthamoeba (pseudodendrite): Coarse, irregular, no terminal bulbs, not ulcerated. Severe pain. CL + tap water history.
  • Healing abrasion: Can look dendritic during re-epithelialisation — but has no terminal bulbs, no reduced sensation, and there's a history of trauma.
HEDS Study — What Every Ophthalmologist Must Know

The Herpetic Eye Disease Study (HEDS) was a landmark multi-centre RCT that settled four key controversies. You will be asked about this on almost every ophthalmology exam:

  1. Topical steroids shorten duration of active HSV stromal keratitis — add prednisolone 1% tapering. But: must always add antiviral cover (topical/oral) to prevent reactivation of epithelial disease.
  2. Oral acyclovir does NOT help active stromal keratitis — the stromal inflammation is immune-mediated (T-cell response to viral antigens), not due to active viral replication. Antivirals target replication, not the immune response.
  3. Oral acyclovir reduces RECURRENCE by 45% (prophylaxis trial) — standard: acyclovir 400 mg BD for patients with ≥2 episodes/year.
  4. Oral acyclovir does NOT prevent HSV iritis in patients with concurrent epithelial disease.

Acanthamoeba Keratitis

Classic Presentation — Three Elements
  1. Contact lens wear + tap water exposure (home-made saline, swimming/showering in lenses, rinsing case with tap water)
  2. Severe pain disproportionate to clinical signs — caused by perineural infiltrates (radial keratoneuritis) along corneal nerve paths
  3. Failure to respond to standard antibiotic/antiviral drops — classically misdiagnosed as HSV initially
💡 Radial keratoneuritis — white linear infiltrates along corneal nerve paths radiating from the stroma — is essentially pathognomonic for Acanthamoeba. If you see this pattern on the slit lamp, start Acanthamoeba treatment immediately without waiting for culture results.

Treatment: Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) 0.02% + propamidine isethionate (Brolene) 0.1% — both drops every hour initially. The double-walled cyst form is resistant to most treatments — this is why treatment continues for months (minimum 3–6 months) to eradicate all cysts.

⚠️ Acanthamoeba is often misdiagnosed as HSV keratitis early on, and topical steroids are given — this dramatically worsens the infection by suppressing the host immune response. Any 'HSV' not responding to ganciclovir in a CL wearer = reconsider Acanthamoeba.

Neurotrophic Keratopathy

Why Loss of Sensation Causes Corneal Disease

Corneal nerves have two functions beyond sensation:

  1. They stimulate the lacrimal reflex — CN V damage → reduced basal tear production
  2. They release neuropeptides (substance P, nerve growth factor/NGF, calcitonin gene-related peptide) that directly support epithelial cell health, proliferation, and adhesion

Without nerve supply, the epithelium becomes fragile, heals poorly, and eventually breaks down spontaneously — even without trauma or infection. The ulcers are characteristically oval, in the interpalpebral zone, painless (hence late presentation), with rolled-in edges.

✂️ Keratoplasty

Modern corneal surgery is layer-targeted — replace only what's diseased

Which Keratoplasty? The Decision Logic

The Fundamental Question

Ask: which layer is diseased? Then replace only that layer.

  • Stroma diseased, endothelium healthy → DALK (replace stroma, keep own DM + endothelium)
  • Endothelium/DM diseased, stroma healthy → DSAEK or DMEK (replace only DM + endothelium)
  • Full-thickness disease, or failed previous graft, or DM breach during DALK → PK

DALK — Anwar's Big-Bubble Technique Explained

The challenge of DALK is removing the stroma right down to DM without perforating it (DM is ~10 µm thick). Anwar's technique solves this elegantly: inject air (or viscoelastic) into the deep stroma using a 27-gauge needle. The air dissects along the natural plane between stroma and Dua's layer → creates a large air bubble (the "big bubble") that lifts the overlying stroma away from DM. Then excise the stroma over the bubble → exposed DM surface → place donor stroma without DM/endothelium.

If the bubble doesn't form → manual layer-by-layer dissection. If DM perforates during dissection → convert to PK intraoperatively.

Why DALK is preferred over PK for KC:

  • No donor endothelium → no endothelial rejection (the only rejection you can't treat medically)
  • Long-term endothelial cell survival better (patient keeps own young, healthy endothelium)
  • Surgical procedure technically closed-eye for most of procedure → safer
  • Corneal donation criteria less strict (endothelial cell count less critical)

Graft Rejection — Recognise and Act Immediately

Why Does Rejection Occur? (Understanding the Immune Privilege)

The cornea is "immune-privileged" — it lacks blood vessels and lymphatics, so donor antigens cannot easily reach regional lymph nodes to sensitise T-cells. However, privilege is not absolute. If the graft becomes vascularised (from previous disease, surgery, or infection), immune cells gain direct access → rejection risk multiplies. This is why a previously vascularised cornea (from herpes, chemical burn, prior failed graft) is "high-risk" and requires systemic immunosuppression (ciclosporin A) after PK.

Corneal Donation — Inclusion and Exclusion

Absolute Contraindications to Donation
  • HIV positive
  • HBsAg positive / Hepatitis C positive
  • Prion disease (CJD, vCJD, FFI) — absolute
  • CNS malignancy (brain tumour, glioma)
  • Leukaemia, lymphoma (haematological malignancy)
  • Death of unknown cause
  • Active systemic sepsis
  • Retinoblastoma or intraocular malignancy
  • Prior LASIK / refractive surgery (unsuitable for optical PK)
Common Exam Traps — CAN Still Donate
  • CMV seropositive — CMV does not transmit via corneal transplantation
  • Age >65 — not absolute contraindication; ECC must be adequate
  • Solid organ cancer (breast, lung, colon) — acceptable if not active at time of death
  • Prior retinal surgery (RD repair, vitrectomy) — acceptable
  • Prior cataract surgery — acceptable (ECC must be assessed)
  • Diabetes — acceptable if ECC adequate
  • Hypertension, ischaemic heart disease — acceptable

👁️ Refractive Surgery

Principles, safety parameters, and complications — what a resident needs before seeing refractive patients

LASIK — How It Works

The Excimer Laser (193 nm)

Operates at 193 nm — this UV wavelength breaks molecular bonds (photoablation) without generating heat. Each pulse removes approximately 0.25 µm of stroma. To correct myopia: ablate more centrally → flatten cornea → reduce refracting power → focal point moves back to retina. For hyperopia: ablate more peripherally → steepen central cornea. For astigmatism: differential ablation along steep meridian.

Why RSB <250 µm Causes Ectasia

The cornea holds its shape through the structural rigidity of its collagen lamellae. If too much stroma is removed (thin RSB), the remaining tissue cannot withstand normal IOP → the posterior cornea slowly bulges forward → post-LASIK ectasia. This is mechanically identical to KC — ectasia driven by mechanical weakness under IOP. This is why forme fruste KC is an absolute contraindication to LASIK — the stroma is already weak before any ablation.

⚠️ Forme fruste KC / posterior elevation >16 µm on Pentacam / BAD-D score >1.6 = ABSOLUTE contraindication to LASIK. This is not a relative contraindication. LASIK on a subclinically ectatic cornea will cause iatrogenic ectasia — vision-threatening and not fully reversible.

Corneal Cross-Linking (CXL)

What CXL Actually Does — The Chemistry

Riboflavin (vitamin B₂) is a photosensitiser. UV-A (365 nm) activates riboflavin → generates reactive oxygen species (ROS). ROS cause new covalent bonds (cross-links) between adjacent collagen molecules in the stroma. Result: stiffer, biomechanically stronger stroma that resists ectasia under IOP.

CXL does NOT reverse ectasia — it halts progression. Some patients get 1–2D reduction in Kmax over months (bonus flattening from stromal remodelling) but this is a side effect, not the goal. Goal = stabilisation.

Standard Dresden Protocol (Steps in Order)
  1. Topical anaesthetic; remove epithelium over 9 mm zone
  2. Riboflavin 0.1% drops every 2 minutes × 30 minutes (stromal soak)
  3. Check: aqueous should appear yellow-green in AC on slit lamp = riboflavin has penetrated to anterior chamber (confirms adequate stromal saturation)
  4. Measure pachymetry after soak — must be ≥400 µm before UV-A delivery. If <400 µm → use hypoosmolar riboflavin to swell the stroma. UV-A can damage endothelium if cornea is too thin.
  5. UV-A 365 nm at 3 mW/cm² × 30 minutes = total dose 5.4 J/cm²
  6. BCL + topical antibiotic until epithelium heals (~5 days)
📌 The 400 µm safety rule: if pachymetry after soaking is <400 µm, the UV-A energy will reach the endothelium and cause endothelial damage. This is why you re-measure AFTER the riboflavin soak, not just at baseline — the soak itself can swell the stroma somewhat.

Intracorneal Ring Segments (ICRS)

Arc Shortening — How ICRS Flatten the Cone

ICRS (Intacs, Kerarings) are semi-circular PMMA inserts placed in mid-peripheral stroma at 75–80% depth via a femtosecond laser tunnel. Mechanism: arc shortening — inserting a semi-circular segment shortens the arc length of that portion of corneal circumference. Like tightening the periphery of a sphere: the peripheral cornea shortens → the central cornea is pushed to flatten (the apex descends). Greater arc length and smaller inner diameter = greater flattening effect.

Reversible — can be removed and cornea returns largely to pre-op shape. Used in KC after CXL has stabilised the cornea: CXL first (stabilise), then ICRS (optically rehabilitate — reduce Kmax, reduce astigmatism → improve contact lens fit or spectacle-correctable vision).
